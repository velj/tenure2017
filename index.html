Teaching Philosophy

“Learning and teaching should not stand on opposite banks and just watch the river flow by; instead, they should embark on a journey down the water. Through an active, reciprocal exchange, teaching can strengthen learning to learn.”

The above quote by Loris Malaguzzi summarizes my teaching philosophy. My philosophy reflects the idea that we are all lifelong learners, therefore, I prefer a classroom setting that encourages learning by both students and teachers. In order for learning to occur a teacher must create an environment that values mutual respect, acceptance of all strengths and weaknesses, and shared goals. While I set expectations high for my students, I am flexible in meeting their individual needs. I see the classroom as a community and we are all responsible for being productive members.

To encourage students to be active participants in their learning, I prefer student-centered teaching strategies, which fosters intrinsic motivation and critical thinking. Through the use of student-centered teaching strategies, I address multiple learning styles and encourage peer to peer learning through sharing experiences to engage in real life connections. Students spend a fair amount of time in small groups and engaging in dialogue. Along with small groups, I use a combination of traditional lecture, multimedia, and application of knowledge into practice. I encourage students to ask questions and provide input in a safe non-judgmental environment to help drive instruction.

I design a course that that allows me to learn about my students interests and utilize teaching strategies that maximizes those interests. I believe that students learn best from teachers who are enthusiastic and knowledgeable. I make myself available to my students and offer outside guidance when necessary. To measure student learning I may use all or a combination of some of the following assessments:

  • Exams
  • Presentations
  • Group Assignments
  • Observation Activities
  • Active Participation
  • Individual Research Papers

I focus on helping students develop the skills needed to think critically, set appropriate goals, and be open to new ideas.

ECE 120:
Child Growth and Development is a foundation course. It is also a prerequisite for the majority of early childhood education (ECE) courses. The course addresses physical, cognitive, social and emotional areas of development from conception through adolescence and the interaction among developmental domains in the developing child. Included in the course is a foundation of theory and research methodology and how they are related to an understanding of child development. I have designed this class to be taught in a unit format covering conception through birth, infants and toddlers, preschoolers, school-agers, and adolescence. This is beneficial for students because it follows a logical sequence and each unit builds upon the other. The main assignments in this class are an infant and toddler observation and a preschool observation. Both assignments now align with state competencies, accreditation, and credentials.

To ensure clarification of the course, I have included a course syllabus, assignment description, and a chart that shows which competencies and accreditation standards the preschool observation assignment meets.

ECE 219:
Early Childhood Science and Math is an activities course that covers the theory and practice of teaching science and math to children from pre-kindergarten to 3rd grade. It focuses on the creation and evaluation of developmentally appropriate science and math activities and materials that encourage young children to use their natural curiosity and interest in objects. Students participate in eight in class labs where they test developmentally appropriate math and science activities and experiments for young children. These labs are hands on and students complete a lab packet that requires them to reflect on ways they will prepare and teach children about the math and science concepts explored during the lab. When the course is concluded, students come away with a repertoire of science and math activities to hopefully implement into their future classroom.

Teaching Squares

Sarah Ruthven and Tim Seitz: Learning Community - Non-Western Art Spring 2017

Two strategies learned:

  1. Sarah reviewed the topics from the previous class and asked students to reflect on ways in which it was connected to the current topic and scheduled reading.
  2. Sarah also encouraged the students to use their books while they engaged in discussion. She was very familiar with the textbook and offered guidance on how the textbook supported her lecture.

Lisa Crizer: ENG 151 - Composition 1 Spring 2017

Two strategies learned:

  1. Lisa utilized canvas to post assignments, submit assignments, and for discussion boards. She had canvas open during most of her class and showed the students what assignments they had left for the semester. Her canvas shell was prepared with assignments for the rest of the semester.
  2. Lisa broke the students into peer groups and delegated each group a topic in which they were to debate. They wrote down their main arguments and then shared them with the class. She then encouraged others to take the opposing side. She was setting the foundation for what was going to be their next paper. The students were engaged and took notes

Lisha Linder: EDU 253 - Children With Exceptionalities Spring 2017

Two strategies learned:

  1. Lisha had a guest speaker who presented about living with autism. Prior to his arrival, Lisha discussed appropriate and inappropriate questions, which she clearly had also done the previous class. She requested the students write their questions down and she either approved them or encouraged them to ask a different question. Her students knew exactly what was expected of them.
  2. When the guest was finished, Lisha challenged her students to reflect on the speakers’ experiences living with autism and the stigma in our society. She also connected his responses to topics discussed in previous classes.

Future implementation of learned strategies:

  1. I appreciated the connections Sarah made to the textbook and I want to begin using mine in the same way. I often assume my students have completed the reading assignment and am quickly proven wrong when I ask them a question they should be able to answer.
  2. I plan to utilize canvas in a more meaningful way. I want to begin setting up my canvas shell prior to the first day of class, so my students know exactly what they will be responsible for. I also plan to invite guest speakers to my classes to connect course content to real-life situations.

ITN provides opportunities for early care and school- age practitioners and educators to pursue professional development through high quality and accessible trainings. ITN trainings are scheduled through our local Child Care Resource & Referral (CCR&R) agency, 4-C: Community Coordinated Child Care. Audience: All Providers Caring for Infants, Toddlers and Twos

Video Self-Assessments

As I participated in new faculty orientation, I discovered the value in video self- assessment. I found this activity to be rewarding and I have gained valuable insight from this experience.

FALL 2015
Date & Time: November 10th at 1:00 p.m.
Class: ECE 115 Early Childhood Education

Strategies developed from the video-tape review and self-assessment:

While reflecting on my video-tape review, I noticed most of my students were engaged in their work. However, not all were. This assessment allowed me to observe myself from the students’ perspective and I noticed that my students would benefit from more active participation. This course is designed as a topics course, but I would like to offer more variety in my teaching strategies in order to connect the topics discussed in class to each other rather than focusing on them as separate entities. I would like to offer more opportunities for cooperative learning to encourage students to assume responsibility for their learning. I would also like to utilize technology and outside resources to connect course content to real-life situations to enhance students’ comprehension of content material. I would also enhance their comprehension by reiterating the main ideas of the lesson to conclude class, which I noticed I did not do. Therefore, I would like to incorporate such a discussion openly between the students and myself. By doing so it will help me identify what topics I need to discuss further.

Results of implementation of strategies:

I noticed that after the students spent more time working cooperatively, they actively participated in class discussion. They felt confident in their contributions and were able to reflect on our discussion. By having the students summarize the main points of the class, I have found that I am more aware of what they actually learned from that class. I can also clarify things they don't quite understand and can connect it to the next class topic.

Suggestions for improvement:

Through observation, I found that the same students actively participate, while others do not contribute. To improve classroom engagement, I’d like to find new ways to help all students to feel confident in the classroom. I want to work on my presentation of material to involve both lecture and conversational teaching to help build classroom participation. While I do believe I do both, I want to pay more attention to the needs of all students to adapt as necessary. I also believe that discussing and showing more research will help the students connect the content to field work.

FALL 2016
Date & Time: October 5th at 1:00 p.m.
Class: ECE 120 Child Growth and Development

Strategies developed from the video-tape review and self-assessment:

This course is taught in a unit-type structure. I cover all stages of growth and development from conception through adolescence. While reflecting on my video-tape review, I realized that I started a new age group and the students were unable to connect the theorists we discussed previously in class to the current age group being discussed. I think it would’ve been beneficial to have done a more in-depth review of the previous age group to give the students a clearer transition. I want to place the students in groups and assign each group a domain of development for the age group we just completed and they will then teach that information back to the class. I feel this will encourage them to review and use their books, since most students do not complete the readings. This is obvious to me, because I opened this class with a few questions regarding their assigned reading and only three students raised their hands to answer. I also think that this activity will allow me to interject when I notice the students are struggling with a topic.

Results of implementation of strategies:

I noticed that after the students began participating in the teach backs, they were able to make more accurate connections to theories. I began adding more short-answer questions on my exams to see if the students knew how to apply the information they learned in class to real-life scenarios.

Suggestions for improvement:

I want to continue to encourage my students to apply theory to practice. I think the best way to do this would be to add a reflection assignment to go along with their field observations. I will require the students to reflect on their observation experiences by connecting the children’s observed development to each of the theories we study.
